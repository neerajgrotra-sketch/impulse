# 07 · Emotional Regulation

> **Purpose:** map what is actually known about how humans regulate emotion — which strategies work, for whom, under what conditions — so Impulse can coach affect honestly instead of dispensing "just reframe it" folk advice. **Status:** Draft v0.1 — 2026-07.

Emotion regulation (ER) sits at the center of decision coaching: most bad decisions people regret are not made in a state of calm — they are made hot, tired, anxious, or ashamed. See `02 Decision Science.md` for how affect distorts choice, and `04 Neuroscience.md` for the underlying stress/arousal machinery. This document owns the *psychology of regulating* that affect: the strategies, their evidence, and their boundary conditions. It obeys `00 Method & Evidence Standard.md` — every substantive claim is tiered, and where the evidence is acute-lab-only we say so in the same sentence.

The single most important honesty note, stated up front: **the vast majority of ER evidence comes from acute laboratory paradigms** — a subject is shown a distressing image or film, told which strategy to use, and self-report or physiology is measured minutes later. Durable, self-initiated, real-world regulation over weeks and months is a *different and much harder* thing, and it is far less well evidenced. Keep that gap in view through everything below. A related honesty note: much of this literature is dominated by trait *self-report* of strategy use (e.g., how often someone says they reappraise), which correlates only modestly with what people actually do in the moment — so "reappraisers have better outcomes" is partly a story about people who *describe themselves* a certain way.

## 1. The organizing map: Gross's process model [A/B]

James Gross's **process model of emotion regulation** is the field's dominant framework, and rightly so — it is less a discovery than a *taxonomy*, and as a taxonomy it is robust and near-universally adopted [A as an organizing scheme]. Its power is that it locates regulation strategies along the **timeline of the emotion-generative process**: an emotion unfolds as *situation → attention → appraisal → response*, and you can intervene at any point.

The five families, in temporal order:

1. **Situation selection** — choosing which situations to enter or avoid (taking a different route home to avoid an ex; not opening the app at midnight). The earliest and often most powerful lever, because it acts before the emotion is generated. Underexploited in most self-help, which fixates on late-stage coping.
2. **Situation modification** — changing the situation once in it (asking to move a hard conversation to a private room; muting a thread).
3. **Attentional deployment** — where you point attention within a situation: **distraction**, concentration, or rumination (rumination is maladaptive attentional deployment — see §7).
4. **Cognitive change** — changing how you appraise the situation or your capacity to cope. **Cognitive reappraisal** lives here (§2).
5. **Response modulation** — acting on the emotional response after it is fully underway: **expressive suppression**, exercise, drugs/alcohol, breathing (§6). The latest and, for suppression specifically, often the costliest lever.

**Antecedent-focused** strategies (1–4) act before the emotional response is fully generated; **response-focused** strategies (5) act after. Gross's central empirical claim — reasonably well-supported [B] — is that *antecedent-focused regulation tends to be more effective and less physiologically costly than response-focused regulation*, because it intervenes before the response has to be paid for.

**What to keep and what to hold loosely.** Keep the map: it is the best available scaffold for *organizing an intervention menu*, and Impulse should use it internally to classify any coaching move. Hold loosely the implied *value ranking* ("earlier is better") — it is a strong central tendency, not a law, and §2/§3 give the counterexamples. The model describes an **extended process** (identification → selection → implementation → monitoring); most failures of regulation in the wild are failures of *identification* (not noticing you need to regulate) and *monitoring* (not switching a strategy that isn't working), not failures of knowing a technique. That has direct product implications: teaching a technique is the easy part.

## 2. Reappraisal vs suppression — the headline finding, and its real boundary [B]

The most-cited result in the ER literature: **cognitive reappraisal** (reinterpreting a situation to change its emotional meaning — "the interviewer isn't hostile, he's testing whether I stay calm") is, on average, associated with *better outcomes* than **expressive suppression** (inhibiting the outward display of emotion while the feeling continues). Reappraisal correlates with lower negative affect, better memory for the situation, more positive social outcomes, and no cardiovascular cost; suppression correlates with sustained or increased physiological arousal, worse memory (because inhibiting is cognitively expensive and steals encoding resources), and — notably — *worse* social outcomes, as interaction partners find suppressors less likable and trustworthy [B]. This is one of the claims `00` flags as comparatively robust, and it is the origin of the pop-psychology slogan.

**But the slogan is the problem.** "Reappraisal good, suppression bad" is an oversimplification the evidence does not license, for three reasons:

- **Sheppes's selection findings — the key boundary condition [B].** Gal Sheppes and colleagues showed that when people are free to *choose* a strategy, their choice tracks emotional intensity in an adaptive way: for **low-intensity** stimuli they prefer **reappraisal** (engage, reframe, integrate); for **high-intensity** stimuli they prefer **distraction** (disengage early, block the input before it is fully processed). And this preference is *sensible* — under high intensity, distraction is often the more effective regulator, because reappraisal requires you to take in and semantically process the very content you are trying to defuse, which is expensive and can backfire when the signal is overwhelming. So the honest statement is: **reappraisal is not universally superior; its advantage is conditional on the emotion being tractable enough to think your way through.** For acute, intense, or trauma-adjacent material, disengagement (distraction, situation selection) can dominate.
- **Controllability.** A parallel and reasonably supported idea: reappraisal and other engagement strategies pay off when the stressor is *uncontrollable* (you cannot change it, so changing your interpretation is the only lever), whereas for *controllable* stressors problem-solving / situation modification is better. Regulating your feelings about a fixable problem instead of fixing it is miscategorized coping.
- **Suppression is not uniformly harmful.** Much of the "suppression is costly" evidence is Western and acute. Cross-cultural work suggests the costs of suppression are **smaller in cultures where emotional restraint is normatively valued** (e.g., some East Asian contexts), consistent with the idea that the harm comes partly from acting *against* one's values/norms rather than from inhibition per se. And brief, tactical suppression ("hold it together for the next ten minutes, process later") is a legitimate move. What the evidence indicts is suppression as a *chronic, default* strategy.
- **The goal is not always to feel good.** Maya Tamir's work on **instrumental emotion regulation** [B/C] shows people sometimes *want* to feel a "negative" emotion because it serves a goal — wanting anger before a confrontation, worry before a task that rewards vigilance. Regulation is goal-directed, not hedonic-by-default. This is a direct warning against a coach that reflexively steers every user toward calm/positive: sometimes the adaptive move is to *permit* or even mobilize an unpleasant emotion. Forced positivity ("just think positive") can backfire, and suppressing a signal that carries real information is a category error.

**Coaching implication.** Impulse must not hand every user "reappraisal" as the answer. The correct move is intensity- and controllability-aware: *is this controllable? → problem-solve/modify. Is it low-intensity and you have bandwidth? → reappraise. Is it high-intensity and you're flooded? → disengage/distract now, reappraise later.* This is directly the Sheppes logic and it is more defensible than the slogan.

A compact grading of the main strategies, so the whole map is visible at once:

| Strategy | Process stage | Evidence | Honest one-line |
|---|---|---|---|
| Situation selection/modification | Antecedent (1–2) | B | Powerful, under-used; acts before the emotion is paid for. |
| Distraction / disengagement | Attention (3) | B | Beats reappraisal under high intensity (Sheppes); can become avoidance if chronic. |
| Rumination | Attention (3) | B | Maladaptive; prospectively predicts depression (Nolen-Hoeksema). |
| Cognitive reappraisal | Cognitive change (4) | B | Healthier *on average*; conditional on tractable intensity; a family, not one skill. |
| Distancing / self-distancing | Cognitive change (4) | B | More robust under load than full reinterpretation (Kross). |
| Affect labeling | Cognitive change (4) | B/C | Real but modest; clarifies, doesn't extinguish. |
| Acceptance / defusion (ACT) | Cognitive change (4) | B | Effective; distinct superiority over CBT is debated. |
| If-then ER (implementation intentions) | Wraps 3–5 | B | Best bet for making a strategy *fire under load*. |
| Expressive suppression | Response (5) | B | Costly as a *chronic default*; tactically fine; culture-dependent. |
| Slow breathing / extended exhale | Response (5) | B/M | Real, modest, acute; a 90-second bridge, not a cure. |
| Mindfulness / decentering | Cross-cutting | B | Moderate for anxiety/depression/stress; hyped beyond that. |

## 3. Reappraisal is a family, not one skill [B]

A refinement that matters for coaching: "reappraisal" bundles several distinct tactics with different profiles.

- **Reinterpretation** (change the meaning of the situation) vs **distancing** (adopt a detached, third-person, or observer perspective — "watch it like a scientist," self-distanced self-talk, Kross's "Solomon's paradox" and third-person self-talk work) [B]. Distancing is often easier to deploy under load than full reinterpretation and has decent support for reducing reactivity and reactive aggression.
- **Temporal distancing** ("will this matter in five years?") and **positive reappraisal / benefit-finding** are further variants with lighter but real support.

The practical point: distancing is frequently the more *teachable* and *robust-under-stress* member of the family, and Impulse should probably lead with distancing prompts rather than demanding full narrative reinterpretation from a flooded user. Effect sizes for reappraisal in the lab are typically **moderate**; its translation into trait-level everyday use is weaker and more variable (see §8).

## 4. Affect labeling — "name it to tame it," real but modest [B/C]

Matthew Lieberman's **affect labeling** work — putting feelings into words ("I'm noticing anger and some fear") — shows that verbalizing an emotion is associated with **reduced amygdala response and increased ventrolateral prefrontal activity**, an incidental/implicit form of regulation (see `04 Neuroscience.md` for the imaging detail and its caveats). This is the science behind Dan Siegel's popular "name it to tame it."

Honest grading: **the effect is real but modest, and it should not be oversold [B for the acute effect; C for the strong self-help version].** Caveats:

- Effects in the lab are **small-to-moderate** and measured on immediate physiological/self-report markers, not on durable mood or decision quality.
- It sometimes works *without* people believing it helped (incidental regulation), which is interesting mechanistically but means subjective relief is not guaranteed.
- The imaging story ("labeling dampens the amygdala") is a real finding but rests on the usual fMRI-reverse-inference caveats; treat the *behavioral* claim as the load-bearing one, not the brain picture.
- Exposure therapy adjuncts suggest labeling can even *aid* fear extinction in some paradigms — promising, but not the same as "naming makes bad feelings go away."

**Coaching implication.** Labeling is a cheap, low-risk, high-frequency micro-intervention — an excellent *default first step* (it also improves emotional granularity over time; see §7) — but Impulse should frame it as "this helps you see the emotion clearly so you can act," not "this will make the feeling stop." Overpromising erodes trust.

## 5. Mindfulness-based interventions — moderate, heterogeneous, hyped [B]

Mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) — MBSR, MBCT, and countless app-delivered variants — have **moderate evidence for reducing anxiety, depression, and stress** [B]. The strongest single result is **MBCT for prevention of depressive relapse** in people with recurrent depression, where it performs comparably to maintenance antidepressants for that specific population — the most solid claim in the mindfulness literature. Meta-analyses of MBIs for anxiety/depression/stress in general populations find **small-to-moderate** effects.

Where the honesty is required:

- **Heterogeneity and weak controls.** A large fraction of the literature uses **waitlist or no-treatment controls**, which inflate effects by capturing expectancy, attention, and demand rather than mindfulness per se. When compared against **active controls** (relaxation, education, other therapies), the specific advantage of mindfulness **shrinks** and often becomes small. An influential AHRQ-style systematic review concluded that evidence was moderate for anxiety/depression/pain and weak or insufficient for many other advertised benefits (attention, sleep, weight, substance use). Treat sweeping "mindfulness fixes everything" claims as **D**.
- **Publication bias and small trials** are documented concerns; effect sizes tend to fall as trial quality rises.
- **Adverse effects are under-reported.** A minority of practitioners — higher among those with trauma histories or intensive practice — experience anxiety, dissociation, or destabilization. The field's long silence on this is a mark against its objectivity.
- **The "hype" tier.** Consumer meditation apps market benefits well beyond what their own (often waitlist-controlled, high-dropout) trials support. The mechanism ("mindfulness works by X") is contested — attention regulation, decentering, exposure, and relaxation are all candidate ingredients and no consensus exists.

**Net.** MBIs are a legitimate, moderately-evidenced tool, best supported for anxiety/stress/depressive-relapse, weaker and hype-laden elsewhere. For Impulse, the transferable *ingredient* is arguably **decentering** (observing thoughts/feelings as transient mental events rather than facts) — which overlaps with distancing (§3) and defusion (§6) — more than "meditate for benefits."

## 6. ACT and DBT — what they add beyond classic CBT is debated

Both are "third-wave" approaches that change the *relationship to* internal experience rather than (only) the content of thoughts.

**ACT (Acceptance & Commitment Therapy)** [B]. Core moves: **cognitive defusion** (seeing thoughts as thoughts — "I'm having the thought that I'll fail" — rather than truths), **acceptance** (willingness to feel unpleasant states without struggle), **contact with the present moment**, **self-as-context**, and crucially **values + committed action** (choosing behavior by what matters, not by what feels comfortable). ACT has solid evidence as an efficacious therapy for anxiety, depression, chronic pain, and more [B].

The honest, contested question: **does ACT outperform classic CBT, and does it work through its claimed mechanisms?** Head-to-head trials and meta-analyses generally find ACT **roughly equivalent** to CBT for most conditions — not clearly superior. So its value proposition is less "it beats CBT" and more "it is another effective route, with a mechanism (psychological flexibility) some patients engage with better, and a values component classic CBT underweights." Mediation evidence that change is *specifically* driven by increased psychological flexibility/defusion is **suggestive but not conclusive** — this is the live debate. Do not claim ACT is mechanistically distinct-and-superior; claim it is effective and adds an explicit values/acceptance emphasis [B, with the mechanism claim held at C].

**DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy) distress-tolerance skills** [B]. DBT as a full program has robust evidence for **borderline personality disorder and chronic self-harm/suicidality** — that is its strongest, well-replicated indication [A/B for that population]. Impulse's interest is narrower: the portable **distress-tolerance and emotion-regulation skills** (e.g., the "TIPP" crisis skills — temperature/cold water, intense exercise, paced breathing, paired muscle relaxation; "radical acceptance"; "opposite action"; "wise mind"). Honest grading: these skills are **plausible and clinically valued, but the evidence for individual skills used as standalone self-help micro-tools is much thinner than the evidence for the full DBT program delivered by trained clinicians.** The cold-water/dive-reflex skill (TIPP) has a plausible physiological basis for acutely dropping arousal but modest controlled evidence for durable benefit. So: borrow the skills as hypotheses to test, not as proven standalone interventions [B for the program, M/C for atomized self-help skills].

**Shared honest point for ACT and DBT.** Both are *packages* validated as packages by trained clinicians over many sessions. Stripping a technique out ("do a defusion exercise," "try opposite action") and delivering it via an app is a reasonable hypothesis but is **not** covered by the trials that made the parent therapy respectable. Impulse must not borrow the therapy's credibility for the app-sized fragment.

## 7. Rumination and its costs [B]; emotional granularity

**Rumination** — the maladaptive form of attentional deployment (§1) — is repetitive, passive, abstract dwelling on the causes and consequences of distress ("why do I always...?"). Susan **Nolen-Hoeksema's** response-styles research established that a ruminative response style **prospectively predicts the onset, severity, and duration of depression**, and partly explains the female-skewed depression prevalence [B]. This is one of ER's better-supported real-world findings *because* it is longitudinal, not just acute-lab.

Key refinements:
- **Brooding vs reflection.** Rumination decomposes into maladaptive **brooding** (passive, self-critical comparison to a standard) and more neutral/adaptive **reflection** (problem-oriented analysis). The pathology loads on brooding. This matters: "thinking about your feelings" is not uniformly bad — the *style* is what predicts harm.
- **Abstract vs concrete** processing (Watkins): abstract "why" rumination is corrosive; concrete "how/what specifically" processing is more adaptive. A coaching lever: convert "why do I always fail" into "what specifically happened and what's one next step."
- **Distinguish from worry** (future-oriented, verbal, anxiety-linked) and from **adaptive reflection**. The transdiagnostic construct is *repetitive negative thinking*.

**Experiential avoidance** — the flip side, and the target ACT (§6) attacks: chronic attempts to escape, suppress, or not-have unwanted internal experiences (thoughts, feelings, sensations). A reasonably supported [B] transdiagnostic risk factor: the *effort to not feel* often amplifies and entrenches the very distress, and narrows life around avoidance (you skip the meeting, the class, the relationship). This reframes the reappraisal/suppression debate one level up: the deeper maladaptive pattern is treating normal aversive affect as an emergency to be eliminated, rather than a state to be carried while acting on what matters. It is also the honest counterweight to a "regulation" product: sometimes the coaching move is *less* regulation, not more.

**Emotional granularity** (Barrett): the ability to represent emotional experience with fine-grained, differentiated concepts ("I feel resentful and disappointed" vs "I feel bad") is associated with better regulation and less maladaptive coping [B, largely correlational]. It connects to affect labeling (§4): labeling repeatedly *builds* granularity over time. This is a plausible medium-term benefit of a coaching habit of naming emotions precisely — offered as a bounded hypothesis, not a proven outcome.

## 8. Implementation-intention-based ER [B]

Gollwitzer's **implementation intentions** (if-then plans; see `08 Behavior Change Models.md` and `00`'s robust list) have been applied to emotion regulation, and this is one of the more promising bridges from lab to durability. The move: pre-commit a regulation strategy to a trigger — *"If I start to feel my chest tighten before the meeting, then I will take three slow breaths and remind myself this is testable"* — so that the regulation is **automatically cued by the situation** rather than requiring in-the-moment executive control (which is exactly what's scarce when you're flooded).

Evidence [B]: adding an implementation-intention wrapper to reappraisal or attentional strategies **improves their deployment and reduces unwanted affect/impulses** in controlled studies, including for disgust, fear, and spider-phobia reactivity, and for controlling impulsive behavior. The mechanism is sound and consistent with the broader if-then literature (among the most robust in behavior change). The limits: still mostly short-horizon studies, effects depend on the plan being well-specified and the cue actually firing, and long-term maintenance is under-studied.

**Why this matters most for Impulse.** ER strategies fail in the wild not because people don't know them but because knowing-in-calm doesn't fire in-heat (the *identification/implementation* failure from §1). If-then planning is the best-evidenced device we have for closing that gap — it converts a technique into a **cued response**. This is arguably the single most actionable, cross-validated finding in this document, and it links ER directly to the goal/planning machinery in `08`.

## 9. Regulatory flexibility beats any single "best" strategy [B/C]

A theme that has hardened over the last decade, and one Impulse should internalize: **there is no context-free "best" regulation strategy — the marker of healthy regulation is *flexibility*, matching strategy to context and switching when it isn't working.** George Bonanno's **regulatory flexibility** model frames this as three abilities: **sensitivity to context** (read what the situation demands), a broad **repertoire** of strategies, and **responsiveness to feedback** (monitor and switch). This dovetails with Sheppes's selection findings (§2) and with Gross's *monitoring* stage (§1).

Honest grading [B for the construct's plausibility and its convergence with selection findings; C for the strong claim that a measured "flexibility" trait reliably predicts outcomes]. The measurement of flexibility is still immature and partly circular (flexibility is sometimes defined by good outcomes, then used to explain them), so treat trait-flexibility scores skeptically. But the *design lesson* is robust and convergent: **do not optimize a user toward one favored technique.** Build repertoire and — most importantly — build the *monitoring habit* of noticing "this isn't working, switch." The under-appreciated corollary: **the same strategy can be adaptive or maladaptive depending on context** (distraction is smart acutely, avoidance if chronic; suppression is fine tactically, corrosive as a default). Rigidity, not any particular strategy, is the reliable villain.

## 10. Physiological down-regulation — slow breathing / extended exhale [B/M]

Slow, paced breathing — especially with a **prolonged exhale** — is the most-hyped acute self-regulation tool of the moment. Grading it honestly requires splitting **mechanism** from **effect size**.

**Mechanism [M, real].** The exhale-lengthening logic is physiologically grounded: exhalation is associated with parasympathetic (vagal) activation and heart-rate deceleration (respiratory sinus arrhythmia), so deliberately extending the exhale and slowing the rate (~5–6 breaths/min, near the "resonance frequency") plausibly nudges the autonomic balance toward calming and raises heart-rate variability acutely. The **"physiological sigh"** (a double inhale through the nose followed by a long exhale) has a genuine basis — it is a spontaneous reflex that reinflates collapsed alveoli — and it is plausible as an acute arousal-down-regulator.

**Effect [B/M, real but modest].** Controlled evidence: slow-breathing and HRV-biofeedback protocols produce **small-to-moderate acute reductions in self-reported stress/anxiety and physiological arousal**. One well-publicized trial found a brief daily "cyclic sighing" practice modestly outperformed comparison practices on mood/arousal over a month — a real but single, short, app-adjacent study, not a settled literature. So: the acute calming effect is real and low-risk; the magnitude is modest and the durable/trait benefits are under-evidenced.

**The overstatement to flag [D for the strong claims].** Popular presentations (much of the Huberman-adjacent canon — see `04`'s treatment of over-extrapolation) imply the physiological sigh is *the fastest and most powerful* way to calm down and frame single-study or mechanistic results as established fact. The honest version: it is *a* cheap, plausible, low-risk acute tool with modest measured effects, not a validated durable regulation system. Do not claim it "resets your nervous system"; that is mechanism-dressed-as-outcome, precisely the failure mode `00 §3.4` warns against.

Note that the specific protocol matters less than commonly implied: "box breathing" (equal 4-4-4-4), coherent/resonance breathing (~6 breaths/min), and cyclic sighing are all plausible variants, and there is no good evidence one is decisively superior. What they share — slowing the rate and not over-breathing — is the likely active ingredient. Beware precise-sounding prescriptions ("exhale exactly twice as long") stated with more confidence than the data supports.

**Coaching implication.** Extended-exhale breathing is a good *response-modulation* micro-tool (§1, step 5) to buy 60–90 seconds of reduced arousal so a *better* strategy (situation selection, distancing, if-then plan) can run. Frame it as a bridge, not a cure.

## 11. What this means for Impulse (design synthesis)

- **Use the process model as an internal menu.** Any regulation move should be classifiable into Gross's five families; bias toward the under-used *antecedent* levers (situation selection/modification) that self-help ignores, not just late-stage coping.
- **Match strategy to intensity and controllability (Sheppes).** Controllable → problem-solve. Low-intensity/uncontrollable → reappraise/distance. High-intensity/flooded → disengage/distract now, reappraise later. Never default everyone to "reframe it."
- **Lead with cheap, robust primitives:** affect labeling (§4) as a first step, distancing over full reinterpretation (§3), and — the strongest bet — **if-then implementation intentions** (§8) to make any chosen strategy fire under load.
- **Offer breathing as a 90-second bridge, not a cure** (§10). Offer mindfulness/decentering and ACT/DBT-derived skills as *bounded, tested* options, never with borrowed clinical authority (§5, §6).
- **Attack rumination structurally** (§7): detect repetitive "why" loops, redirect to concrete "what/how-next," and build granularity through repeated precise labeling.
- **Prefer decentering/distancing as the transferable core** across mindfulness, ACT defusion, and reappraisal (§3, §5, §6): the common ingredient — seeing thoughts/feelings as transient events rather than facts — is more robust and teachable than any branded package.
- **Don't default to calm/positive** (§2, §7): regulation is goal-directed. Sometimes the adaptive move is to permit or mobilize an unpleasant emotion (Tamir), and pushing acceptance/action over avoidance can beat pushing "feel better." A coach that always down-regulates can reinforce experiential avoidance.
- **Build flexibility and the switch-habit, not a favorite technique** (§9): rigidity is the reliable villain; monitor "is this working?" and switch.
- **Respect the acute-lab gap** (§8, §12): treat every technique as a hypothesis to validate against *this user's* durable outcomes, not as a delivered promise.

## 12. Where the evidence is weak / what Impulse must not assume

- **Do not assume acute-lab results transfer to durable, self-initiated real-world regulation.** This is the field's central weakness. Almost everything above was measured minutes after an instructed strategy in a lab; sustained, unprompted regulation across weeks is a *different construct* and is thinly evidenced. Impulse must measure its own outcomes and not assume the lab effect size is the field effect size.
- **Do not preach "reappraisal good, suppression bad."** The advantage of reappraisal is conditional (intensity, controllability, culture, and it is a family of tactics). Under high intensity, distraction/disengagement can win (Sheppes). Chronic default suppression is the real problem, not tactical inhibition.
- **Do not overstate affect labeling.** "Name it to tame it" is real but modest [B/C]; it clarifies, it does not reliably extinguish feeling. The amygdala-dampening brain story carries fMRI-inference caveats.
- **Do not oversell mindfulness.** Moderate for anxiety/depression/stress and solid for depressive-relapse prevention [B]; much of the wider literature uses weak controls, shows shrinking effects under active comparison, under-reports adverse events, and is marketed far past its evidence.
- **Do not borrow clinical credibility for app-sized fragments.** ACT and DBT are validated as *multi-session, clinician-delivered packages*. Whether ACT beats CBT, and whether it works through its named mechanisms, is *debated* — do not assert distinct superiority. Atomized DBT skills as standalone self-help are plausible but under-evidenced (M/C).
- **Do not claim breathing "resets the nervous system."** The mechanism is real [M]; acute effects are real but modest [B]; durable/trait benefits and the strongest popular claims are unproven [D]. It is a bridge tool.
- **Do not assume any technique fires when it's needed.** The dominant real-world failure is *identification and implementation*, not ignorance of techniques. Without an if-then cue (§8) or environmental support, taught strategies often don't deploy under exactly the arousal they're meant for. Teaching ≠ regulating.
- **Do not assume "regulate more" is the goal.** Instrumental emotion goals (Tamir) [B/C] and the experiential-avoidance literature [B] both warn that suppressing or eliminating aversive affect can be the wrong target; sometimes carrying the feeling while acting on values is healthier. Impulse must not equate emotional coaching with emotional dampening.
- **Trait self-report ≠ in-the-moment behavior.** Much of the correlational ER literature rests on questionnaires about habitual strategy use, which map only loosely onto what people do when actually aroused; do not treat "self-described reappraisers do better" as proof that teaching reappraisal produces the outcome.
- **"Best strategy" thinking is a trap.** The evidence points to *flexibility* (context-matching + switching), not a rankable list of techniques [B/C]; but flexibility measurement is immature and partly circular, so do not over-formalize a "flexibility score."
- **Individual differences are large and under-modeled.** Strategy effectiveness varies by person, context, culture, and emotion type; population averages hide this. Any recommendation is a starting hypothesis for a given user, not a settled prescription.
