# 06 · Human Needs

> **Purpose:** compare the major theories of *what humans fundamentally need* — Maslow, Self-Determination Theory, evolutionary reframes, positive psychology, and minor frames — and grade each by how much the evidence actually supports it, so Impulse can ground coaching in needs that are real rather than needs that are merely famous. **Status:** Draft v0.1 — 2026-07.

This document owns the **comparison of needs theories**. The *dynamics* of motivation — how intrinsic/extrinsic drives operate, identity, purpose, self-efficacy — live in `01 Human Motivation`; we reference them but do not re-derive them. Obeys `00 Method & Evidence Standard` (tiers, anti-fabrication, §4 watchlist).

## 1. Why "needs" is a contested category

Before comparing theories we should be honest that "need" is doing heavy and inconsistent work across them. Three different meanings are in play, and much of the apparent disagreement is theories talking past each other:

- **Deficit needs** — states whose *absence* causes harm or dysfunction (Maslow's lower rungs, physiological survival). Testable by deprivation.
- **Psychological nutriments** — conditions whose satisfaction *promotes* thriving and whose thwarting *undermines* it, independent of felt desire (SDT's usage). Testable by within-person well-being covariation.
- **Evolved motivational systems** — recurrent adaptive problems that shaped domain-specific drives (evolutionary usage). Harder to test directly; risk of post-hoc storytelling.

A theory can be strong on one meaning and empty on another. Keeping these apart is the single most clarifying move in this literature. When someone says "belonging is a need," ask: deprivation-harmful, thriving-promoting, or evolved-system? The answer changes what evidence counts.

**How to read the tiers below.** The frameworks differ less in *what* they name than in *how testably* they name it — and the tier tracks the method, not the fame. The one-screen orientation:

| Framework | Core structure | Chief evidential basis | How falsifiable | Tier (as a whole) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Maslow** | 5-level prepotent ladder | Clinical/biographical exemplars; later survey tests | Ordering claim testable — and it fails | **D** (ladder) / B (needs exist) |
| **SDT** | 3 non-hierarchical nutriments | Lab + field + longitudinal + within-person diary, multi-lab | Nutriment logic is testable and tested | **B** |
| **Evolutionary** | Evolved domain-specific systems | Comparative, cross-cultural, a-priori-predicted patterns | Strong when predictions precede data; weak when post-hoc | **C** (B for belonging) |
| **Positive psych** | Flourishing dimensions (PERMA / Ryff) | Surveys + intervention trials | Factor structure & intervention effects testable | **C** (B for Ryff description) |
| **Max-Neef / ERG / self-worth** | Various | Conceptual; some correlational | Mostly not a predictive program | conceptual / **C–D** |

The rest of the document defends each row and, crucially, shows where the rows *agree on content* despite disagreeing on architecture (§7).

## 2. Maslow's hierarchy — dominant culturally, weak empirically

**The claim.** Human needs form a five-level prepotent ladder — physiological → safety → love/belonging → esteem → self-actualization — where lower needs must be *substantially satisfied* before higher ones activate and motivate.

**Cultural status vs evidential status.** No needs framework is more famous. The pyramid is ubiquitous in management training, nursing curricula, and popular self-help — despite Maslow himself never drawing a pyramid (the triangle is a later textbook artifact). This is a textbook case of the §00 warning that **popularity is not evidence**.

**What holds up [B].** That the *content* domains are real and recur — physiological survival, safety, connection, esteem/competence, growth — is defensible and echoed by nearly every later framework (see §7 convergence). The list of human concerns Maslow named was a genuine contribution.

**What does not [D].** The **strict prepotent ordering** — the load-bearing, distinctive claim — is not empirically supported (per §4 watchlist: **D** for the strict ladder, **B** that the needs exist). Failures:

- People routinely pursue esteem, meaning, and connection while lower needs are unmet — art, love, and self-sacrifice under privation are common, not anomalous.
- The gating mechanism ("X% of a lower need must be met before the next activates") was never operationalized in a testable way, and attempts to find rank-ordered activation have largely failed.
- Self-actualization is defined loosely enough to resist falsification, and Maslow's evidence for it was a small, non-representative set of hand-picked exemplars — not a sampling design.

**A late complication.** Maslow himself, late in life, floated a sixth level above self-actualization — **self-transcendence** (purpose beyond the self). It is often dropped from the textbook triangle. We note it because it foreshadows the *meaning/purpose* dimension that recurs in §7 — but it inherits all the ordering and operationalization problems above and adds no independent evidence.

**Why the ladder is so sticky despite being wrong.** The pyramid persists because it is *memorable, intuitive, and administratively convenient* (it licenses simple sequencing: "handle basics first"). None of these is an evidential virtue. Impulse is exactly the kind of product that will be tempted to ship the pyramid as a clean onboarding narrative; §00's discipline exists to resist precisely that temptation.

**The best data we have.** Tay & Diener's analysis of the **Gallup World Poll** (large, cross-national, many countries) is the most-cited empirical test. Its takeaway is the nuanced one Impulse should carry: **the needs matter roughly universally and independently predict aspects of well-being — but they are not satisfied in Maslow's strict order.** People derive benefit from social and esteem needs even when basic needs are unmet; the needs behave more like a set of largely independent contributors than a staircase. Order is not prepotent; presence is broadly universal.

**Verdict.** Keep the *inventory*, discard the *ladder*. Impulse must never sequence coaching as "fix survival/safety before we can address growth." That is the folk version and it is wrong.

## 3. Self-Determination Theory — the best-evidenced needs framework [B]

**The claim (Deci & Ryan and the SDT tradition).** Three **basic psychological needs** are universal nutriments for motivation, well-being, and healthy functioning:

- **Autonomy** — experiencing one's behavior as self-endorsed and volitional (not "independence" — see §3.2).
- **Competence** — feeling effective, able to meet optimal challenges and grow.
- **Relatedness** — feeling connected to and cared about by others.

**Why it earns [B] — the strongest tier in this document.** SDT is distinguished from its rivals by *how* its claims are supported:

- **Convergent, multi-method evidence.** Lab experiments, field studies, longitudinal designs, and diary/within-person studies converge: satisfaction of the three needs predicts higher-quality motivation and well-being; *thwarting* predicts ill-being and controlled motivation. The within-person daily-diary work is especially persuasive — on days a person's needs are more met, that same person reports better functioning, which controls for stable individual differences.
- **A generative research program**, not a static taxonomy — spawning sub-theories (Cognitive Evaluation, Organismic Integration, Basic Psychological Needs) that make distinct, tested predictions.
- **Mechanistic coherence with `01`.** SDT undergirds the intrinsic/extrinsic distinction and the **overjustification effect** (tangible, expected rewards on already-interesting tasks can undermine intrinsic motivation — bounded **B** per §4). Autonomy-support as a driver of *sustained* motivation is on §00's short list of comparatively robust findings. Coordinate with `01` for the motivation dynamics; `05 Coaching Science` for autonomy-support in the coaching relationship.

**Why not [A].** Effect sizes are heterogeneous and moderate; measurement of the needs relies heavily on self-report (need-*satisfaction* scales, with the risk that people report satisfaction and well-being through the same rosy lens — a shared-method confound); causal direction in field studies is often inferred rather than manipulated; and the cross-cultural universality claim (§3.2) is genuinely contested. Real boundary conditions exist — hence **B, not A**. What lifts it above its rivals is not that it escaped these problems but that it *addressed* several of them head-on: the within-person diary designs blunt the direction-of-causation and individual-difference confounds in a way no other needs theory attempted.

**One refinement worth carrying: satisfaction ≠ frustration.** The SDT tradition increasingly treats need *thwarting/frustration* as a distinct construct from mere low *satisfaction* — active frustration (feeling controlled, inadequate, rejected) predicts ill-being and defensiveness more sharply than the simple absence of satisfaction does. For coaching this matters: *removing* what frustrates a need is not the same intervention as *adding* what satisfies it, and the former may be the higher-leverage move. (Ties to `05` on rupture-repair and to `01` on controlled vs autonomous motivation.)

### 3.1 Where SDT beats Maslow, structurally
- **Non-hierarchical.** All three needs operate continuously; none gates the others. This matches the Tay & Diener picture far better than a ladder.
- **Falsifiable nutriment logic.** "Thwart need X, functioning drops" is a testable prediction, and it has been tested.
- **Process, not endpoint.** SDT describes ongoing conditions for thriving rather than a rare summit ("self-actualization") most people never reach.
- **Small, universal set.** Three needs (vs Maslow's five levels, Ryff's six dimensions, Max-Neef's nine) — parsimony that survived testing rather than a longer list defended by fame. A shorter, load-bearing set is also easier to operationalize in a product without over-fitting.

### 3.2 The cross-cultural debate (the live fight)
The serious objection: is **autonomy** a universal need or a Western/individualist value smuggled in as one? Collectivist-culture critics argue that valorizing autonomy privileges independence over interdependence.

The SDT reply (Chirkov, Ryan, and colleagues) turns on a distinction Impulse must preserve: **autonomy ≠ independence.** Autonomy means acting from volitional self-endorsement; one can *autonomously endorse* interdependent, collectivist, or duty-bound choices. On this reading, cross-cultural data show autonomy still predicts well-being in collectivist samples *when measured as volition rather than independence.* Critics counter that the construct is then defined broadly enough to be hard to falsify. The honest status: **relatedness and competence travel with little dispute; autonomy-as-volition is well-supported but its universality remains actively argued** — hence the framework as a whole sits at **B**, not **A**. (Related: the Iyengar & Lepper choice-overload findings suggest the *value placed on personal choice* is culturally moderated — a caution against exporting a Western choice-maximizing frame.)

## 4. Evolutionary reframes — right about origins, exposed to just-so stories

Evolutionary psychology reframes needs as **evolved motivational systems** solving recurrent adaptive problems (survival, self-protection, affiliation, status, mate acquisition/retention, kin care). Two strands matter.

**Kenrick's "renovated pyramid" [C].** Kenrick and colleagues kept Maslow's pyramid shape but re-founded it on evolutionary/life-history logic: lower levels (immediate physiological, self-protection, affiliation, status/esteem) are retained and overlap rather than being superseded, and — the pointed move — **self-actualization at the apex is replaced by reproductive goals: mate acquisition, mate retention, and parenting.** Strengths: it grounds the "why" Maslow lacked (Maslow described *that* we have these needs, evolution proposes *why*), and it correctly makes lower needs recurrent rather than "completed." Weaknesses: swapping the capstone was controversial even among evolutionary psychologists, and the pyramid form drags along the ordering baggage §2 rejects. Kenrick's model also imports **life-history theory** — the idea that individuals calibrate toward faster or slower strategies (short- vs long-term orientation) depending on early environmental cues like harshness and unpredictability. That calibration idea is more testable than the pyramid reshuffle and connects to how people weight immediate vs delayed outcomes (relevant to `02 Decision Science`'s temporal discounting). **C: a plausible reframe, not a validated ordering.**

**Baumeister & Leary's "need to belong" [B].** The strongest single contribution from this quarter: a well-argued case that the drive to form and maintain lasting positive relationships is a **fundamental human motivation** meeting the criteria for a need — pervasive across cultures, easily satisfied, producing broad cognitive/emotional effects, and whose thwarting reliably produces ill-effects. This is well-evidenced and converges directly with SDT's **relatedness** (§7). Belonging thwarted (social exclusion, loneliness) has robust negative consequences; the *general* finding is solid **B**.
> **Watchlist tie-in (§4):** the sub-claim that "rejection is *literally* the same as physical pain" is only **C** — an influential neuroimaging finding whose strong "same neural system" interpretation was contested on reanalysis. The *need to belong* is solid; the *shared-pain-circuitry* gloss is not. Keep them separate. (Mechanism detail: `04 Neuroscience`.)

**What the evolutionary lens adds that the others cannot.** Two things, both about *why* rather than *what*. First, it predicts that needs are **domain-specific and context-triggered**, not a single fungible drive — the system that governs self-protection is not the one that governs status, and each switches on under different cues. This is a sharper, more testable structure than "a hierarchy of needs," and it dovetails with dual-process and threat literatures in `02 Decision Science` and `04 Neuroscience`. Second, it explains **why needs conflict** — evolved systems were selected for reproductive fitness, not for coherent well-being, so trade-offs (status vs. affiliation, short-term vs. long-term) are expected, not pathological. That reframing is genuinely useful for a decision coach: some "irrational" pulls are ancient systems firing on modern cues.

**The generic risk [C].** Evolutionary need-claims are prone to **just-so stories** — post-hoc adaptive narratives that fit any observation and predict nothing new. The discipline's defensible core uses *a priori, testable* predictions (e.g., sex-differentiated or context-triggered patterns predicted in advance); its weak fringe reverse-engineers a fitness story for whatever was observed. Impulse should treat evolutionary framing as a source of **origin explanations and hypotheses**, valuable for saying *why* a need recurs, but should not treat an adaptive story as evidence that a coaching intervention will work (per §00 rule 4: mechanism ≠ intervention).

## 5. Positive psychology — useful vocabulary, contested magnitudes

Positive psychology reframes the question from "what's absent/broken" to "what constitutes flourishing." Two models dominate.

**Seligman's PERMA.** Flourishing decomposes into **P**ositive emotion, **E**ngagement, **R**elationships, **M**eaning, **A**ccomplishment. As a *descriptive vocabulary* for what a good life contains it is useful and popular. As a *theory*, it is weaker: factor-analytic work repeatedly finds PERMA's components hard to distinguish empirically from general well-being / life satisfaction — the elements are so correlated that whether PERMA measures five things or one relabeled thing is unresolved. Treat as a helpful **framework/checklist [C as a distinct five-factor structure]**, not a validated causal model.

**Ryff's eudaimonic well-being [B for the descriptive model].** Ryff's psychological well-being model specifies six dimensions — **autonomy, environmental mastery, personal growth, positive relations, purpose in life, self-acceptance.** It is better-grounded psychometrically than PERMA and its dimensions overlap strikingly with SDT (autonomy, mastery≈competence, positive relations≈relatedness — see §7). Debate persists over dimensional structure (how cleanly the six separate) and the hedonic-vs-eudaimonic distinction's empirical payoff, but as a description of well-being's components it holds up reasonably. **B** as a descriptive taxonomy; the sharp hedonic/eudaimonic split is more contested **[C]**.

**The hedonic counterpoint — Diener's subjective well-being.** Predating and sitting alongside PP is Diener's **subjective well-being (SWB)** tradition: well-being operationalized as *life satisfaction plus the balance of positive over negative affect*. It is the best-*measured* member of this family (validated scales, huge cross-national datasets — the same Gallup infrastructure §2 relies on) and is more agnostic about *which* needs matter, asking instead how satisfied people report being. Its limitation is the mirror image of its strength: it describes the *outcome* (felt well-being) without a strong theory of the *conditions* that produce it — which is exactly the gap SDT's needs fill. PERMA and Ryff can be read as attempts to specify the *contents* of a good life that SWB only measures the felt result of. For Impulse: SWB-style measures are useful *outcome* instruments; they are not a needs theory.

**The intervention effect-size debate (important, and Impulse must be honest about it).** Early meta-analyses (Sin & Lyubomirsky) reported encouraging effects for positive-psychology interventions (gratitude, best-possible-self, acts of kindness). But:
- Later, more rigorous meta-analyses (e.g., Bolier and colleagues) found **notably smaller effects**, shrinking further after accounting for **study quality and publication bias.**
- Reanalyses correcting for small-sample bias and risk-of-bias pushed several headline effects toward **small or negligible**.
- Some individual techniques (certain gratitude and best-possible-self exercises) retain modest, replicable short-term effects; sweeping "these exercises reliably raise lasting happiness" claims do not survive scrutiny.

**Why the effects shrank — the mechanism of the overclaim.** This is instructive for Impulse's own future evaluation discipline, so it is worth naming the *why*, not just the *what*: early PP trials were often small, unblinded, run by advocates, compared against weak control conditions (waitlist rather than active placebo), and selectively published. Each of these inflates apparent effects; correcting for them (larger samples, active controls, pre-registration, bias-adjustment) is exactly what pulled the estimates down. The pattern — enthusiastic early effects deflating under methodological rigor — recurs across the §00 watchlist (ego depletion, grit, growth mindset's transformative version) and should be treated as the *default expectation* for any feel-good intervention until proven otherwise.

**Verdict.** Positive psychology contributed durable *vocabulary* (flourishing, engagement, meaning, eudaimonia) and a few modestly-effective exercises, wrapped in a popular literature that **overstated intervention magnitudes.** Mine it for constructs; do not import its intervention effect sizes at face value. Overlaps heavily with `05 Coaching Science` on what techniques actually move outcomes.

## 6. Other frames worth noting briefly

**Max-Neef — fundamental human needs.** A development economist's taxonomy: a small set of **finite, universal, non-hierarchical** needs (subsistence, protection, affection, understanding, participation, leisure, creation, identity, freedom) distinguished sharply from **satisfiers** — the culturally-variable ways needs are met. The valuable, portable idea: **needs are universal; satisfiers are cultural**, and one satisfier can serve or violate several needs at once. Max-Neef also distinguished *pseudo-satisfiers* and *violators* — things that appear to meet a need while actually undermining it (a directly relevant caution: a coaching feature could look like it satisfies competence while really feeding contingent self-worth, §6 below). Explicitly rejects hierarchy (aligns with §7). Not an empirical research program — a conceptual/normative framework from development studies — so it carries no tier for *predictive* claims; useful as a lens, not evidence.

**ERG / Alderfer.** A reworking of Maslow into three tiers — **Existence, Relatedness, Growth** — with two improvements: needs can operate *simultaneously* (not strictly gated), and a **frustration-regression** principle (blocked at a higher need, people re-intensify a lower one). More flexible than Maslow and historically influential in organizational psychology, but the empirical base is thin and dated; treat as a **conceptual refinement of Maslow [C]**, not independently strong evidence. Note it foreshadows SDT's relatedness and growth/competence.
> The frustration-regression idea is worth flagging as a *hypothesis* for coaching: when growth is blocked (a stalled goal), people may defensively retreat toward lower-order comforts — a pattern that rhymes with SDT need-frustration (§3) and with relapse dynamics in `03 Habit Formation`. Plausible and useful to watch for; not established as a mechanism.

**Self-worth / self-esteem theories — and the movement's overreach [D for the strong causal claim].** A cluster treating the maintenance of self-worth as a core motive (self-worth as fundamental; Crocker's **contingencies of self-worth** — *what* people stake their worth on matters more than global level). The research-grade version is defensible: *domains* of contingent self-worth predict distinct vulnerabilities. But the **1980s–90s self-esteem movement** — that raising self-esteem would raise achievement and reduce social problems — is a cautionary tale directly relevant to Impulse's skepticism: a major review (Baumeister and colleagues) concluded **high self-esteem does not reliably cause better performance or behavior; the correlations are largely self-esteem *following* success, plus confounds.** Worse, some interventions praising the self decoupled from effort produced no benefit or backfired (cf. the person-vs-process praise distinction that resurfaces in growth-mindset work, §00). **D for "boost self-esteem → better outcomes"; the contingencies-of-self-worth refinement is more like C/B.** Impulse must not build a "make users feel good about themselves" mechanic on this discredited causal story. A further finding sharpens the caution: *inflated or threatened* self-esteem (esp. its unstable/narcissistic form) is associated with defensiveness and aggression when challenged — so naive self-esteem-boosting is not merely inert but can be counterproductive. The defensible design goal is competence and autonomous self-endorsement (SDT), not global self-regard.

## 7. Synthesis — where the theories converge (flag for `10`)

Strip the incompatible *architectures* (ladder vs nutriments vs evolved systems vs flourishing dimensions) and a striking **convergence on content** remains. Independent traditions, using different methods and vocabularies, keep rediscovering the same small set:

| Recurring need | Maslow | SDT | Evolutionary | Ryff / PP | Max-Neef / ERG |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Competence / mastery / growth** | esteem, self-actualization | **competence** | status, skill-building | environmental mastery, personal growth, Accomplishment | Growth (ERG); understanding/creation |
| **Relatedness / belonging** | love/belonging | **relatedness** | **need to belong**, affiliation, kin care | positive relations, Relationships | Relatedness; affection/participation |
| **Autonomy / volition / self-direction** | (implicit in self-actualization) | **autonomy** | weaker fit | autonomy | freedom/identity |
| **Meaning / purpose** | self-actualization | (integration/internalization) | reproductive & kin goals give proximate meaning | purpose in life, Meaning | identity/transcendence |
| **Security / safety** | physiological, safety | (background condition) | self-protection, survival | (environmental mastery) | subsistence, protection |

**A skeptical check on the convergence itself.** We should ask whether this agreement is *independent discovery* or *shared lineage* — later theorists read Maslow and each other, so overlap could be intellectual inheritance rather than triangulation on truth. Two things push back: the frameworks arose from **different methods and disciplines** (clinical observation, experimental social psychology, evolutionary biology, survey epidemiology, development economics), and the convergent needs also show up in **atheoretical, bottom-up survey data** (the Gallup World Poll's needs emerge from the data, not from a theorist's list). Lineage explains some overlap; it does not explain agreement across methods that different fields would not share. The convergence is real but should be held as *well-triangulated content*, not proof of a correct architecture.

**The load-bearing convergence for Impulse:** **competence/mastery, relatedness/belonging, and autonomy/volition recur across essentially every serious framework** — and they are exactly the three the best-evidenced theory (SDT, **B**) names as basic. This triangulation across independent traditions is stronger than any single framework's endorsement. Meaning/purpose is a strong fourth candidate but is defined more variably and measured more softly, so we flag it as convergent-but-fuzzier. **`10 Research Synthesis` should carry this three-(to-four-)need convergence as a foundational, cross-disciplinary finding**, while inheriting the caveat that convergence on *content* does not license any specific *architecture* or intervention.

**Where they genuinely conflict (do not paper over this).** Convergence on content is real but partial; three live disagreements remain and belong in `09 Contradictions`:
- **Architecture.** Gated ladder (Maslow) vs. simultaneous nutriments (SDT) vs. context-switched domain-specific systems (evolutionary). These are not reconcilable framings; they make different predictions about *sequencing* and *conflict* between needs. SDT's non-gated view has the best support.
- **Is autonomy fundamental or Western?** Unresolved (§3.2). If the skeptics are partly right, a coach exporting autonomy-maximization could misfire in interdependent contexts.
- **Endpoint vs. process.** Maslow and PERMA aim at a *state* (self-actualization, flourishing) you reach; SDT and Max-Neef describe *ongoing conditions* you maintain. The latter framing fits a lifelong-coaching product far better and is better evidenced — but that is a reasoned preference, not a settled fact.

**The bridge back to §00's question ("what causes better decisions over time?").** Needs theory is not directly a decision theory, and we should not overclaim the link. But two connections are defensible and worth handing to `01`/`10`:
- **Need-supportive conditions predict *sustained* self-regulation.** Autonomy-supportive contexts improving durable motivation is on §00's short list of robust findings. A decision practice a person has *volitionally endorsed* (autonomy) and feels *able to execute* (competence) is more likely to persist than one imposed — persistence is what turns a good decision into a better *trajectory* of decisions.
- **Need-*frustration* degrades decision quality.** Thwarted needs (feeling controlled, inadequate, rejected) map onto the threat/defensive states that `02 Decision Science` and `04 Neuroscience` associate with narrowed, short-horizon, reactive choice. So supporting needs is plausibly *upstream* of better decisions — a hypothesis (**M/C**), not a proven causal chain, and flagged as such for `10`.
- **Level-of-analysis caution.** Needs theory operates on a *slow* timescale (background conditions for thriving over months and years); most decision science operates on a *fast* one (the moment of choice). Impulse spans both, and the two should not be collapsed — a need is not a bias, and satisfying a need is not the same category of intervention as debiasing a choice. `10` should keep these levels distinct when it assembles the foundation.

**Design implication (for `01`/`05`/`10`, not asserted as proven here):** coaching that supports autonomy (self-endorsed goals, not imposed ones), builds competence (optimal challenge, visible progress), and strengthens relatedness (alliance, connection) is betting on the best-supported needs. Meaning is a plausible addition. Sequencing needs like a Maslow ladder is not supported and should be avoided.

## 8. Where the evidence is weak / what Impulse must not assume

- **Do not assume Maslow's ordering.** The strict prepotent ladder is **D** (§4 watchlist). Never sequence coaching as "satisfy lower needs before addressing higher ones." The Tay & Diener picture — needs universal, order not prepotent — governs.
- **Do not overclaim SDT's universality.** SDT is our strongest framework (**B**), but autonomy's cross-cultural status is genuinely debated. Preserve **autonomy ≠ independence**; do not export a Western choice-maximizing frame (Iyengar & Lepper caution). It is **B**, not **A** — build with humility.
- **Do not treat evolutionary stories as intervention evidence.** Origin explanations (**C**, sometimes B for belonging) explain *why* a need exists; they do not show a coaching feature will work (§00 rule 4). Guard against just-so stories.
- **Do not conflate belonging with shared pain circuitry.** Need to belong is **B**; "rejection literally *is* physical pain" is **C** and contested on reanalysis (§4). Keep the strong need and the weak mechanism-claim separate.
- **Do not import positive-psychology intervention effect sizes at face value.** PERMA is a **checklist, not a validated five-factor causal model [C]**; PP-intervention effects **shrink substantially** under quality control and publication-bias correction. A few exercises retain modest short-term effects; "reliably raises lasting happiness" does not survive scrutiny.
- **Do not build on the self-esteem-movement premise.** "Raise self-esteem → better outcomes" is **D**; high self-esteem largely *follows* success rather than causing it. The defensible version is contingencies-of-self-worth (**C/B**), not global self-esteem boosting.
- **Do not treat "meaning/purpose" as a fourth need on par with the other three.** It recurs across frameworks (§7) but is defined inconsistently (self-transcendence, purpose-in-life, reproductive goals, narrative coherence) and measured softly. Real and worth supporting, but its construct and evidence are weaker than competence/relatedness/autonomy — carry it as a *strong candidate*, not an established basic need.
- **What we *can* lean on:** the cross-framework convergence on **competence, relatedness, and autonomy** (with meaning as a softer fourth) is the most trustworthy claim in this document — precisely because independent traditions with different methods keep landing on it. Even here: convergence on *content* is strong; any specific *architecture* or *intervention* remains a hypothesis to be tested, not a foundation.

**Bottom line for the corpus:** of every needs framework, only SDT earns a solid **B**, and the safest claim in the whole field is the *cross-method convergence* on **competence, relatedness, and autonomy** (meaning a softer fourth). Build coaching on those three; treat Maslow's ladder, PP intervention magnitudes, and self-esteem-boosting as things to *avoid assuming*, not foundations.
