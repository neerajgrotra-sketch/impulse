## Section 2 · Contradictions — between disciplines (not between authors)

Only genuine *cross-disciplinary* tensions are listed, each with what disagrees, why, current evidence, and residual uncertainty.

**2.1 Identity as cause (habit/popular) vs behavior→identity (psychology) vs value-insensitive habit (neuroscience).**
- *What disagrees:* Popular habit framing (Clear, in 03 §2.2) treats identity as the driver of behavior; self-perception theory (03 §3) says identity is largely *inferred from* behavior; neuroscience (04 §6) shows established habits run in a system *insensitive to current value/identity* altogether.
- *Why:* different levels of analysis — narrative self-concept vs associative learning vs cortico-striatal control.
- *Current evidence:* behavior→identity is better supported `[B]`; identity→durable-habit is thin `[C]`; cued routines can bypass value entirely `[B]`.
- *Uncertainty:* the causal weight of each direction in everyday change is unresolved.

**2.2 Motivation-as-cause (older motivation psychology) vs implementation/volition (decision + habit science).**
- *What disagrees:* one tradition explains behavior by strength of wanting; the enactment literature shows intention weakly predicts action and volitional/structural mechanisms close the gap.
- *Why:* motivation research often measured intention and self-report; enactment research measured behavior.
- *Current evidence:* motivation is necessary but insufficient; volitional levers dominate `[A/B]` (Webb & Sheeran; Gollwitzer & Sheeran).
- *Uncertainty:* how much *minimum* motivation is required before volitional tools work.

**2.3 Recovery science vs streak/gamification convention.**
- *What disagrees:* recovery evidence (03 §6) says construing a lapse as a broken commitment (abstinence-violation effect) drives full relapse; the streak convention makes an unbroken chain the salient goal — engineering exactly that construal.
- *Why:* product convention optimizes short-term engagement; recovery science optimizes resumption.
- *Current evidence:* recovery/self-compassion `[B]`; streak efficacy for durable habit is unsupported `[C/D]` and plausibly conflicts with overjustification (01 §3).
- *Uncertainty:* no head-to-head trial of streak framing vs recovery framing, especially in apps.

**2.4 Choice-architecture/nudge optimism (behavioral economics) vs autonomy emphasis (motivation/coaching).**
- *What disagrees:* nudging shapes behavior via the choice environment, sometimes below awareness; SDT/coaching holds that autonomy-supportive, self-endorsed action is what sustains.
- *Why:* different outcome targets — immediate compliance vs durable internalized motivation.
- *Current evidence:* defaults robust `[A]`; broad nudge effects small and publication-bias-inflated `[C]`; autonomy-support `[B]`. Covert nudging may win the moment while eroding internalization (overjustification, 01 §3).
- *Uncertainty:* whether autonomy-preserving nudges keep their effect.

**2.5 Dual-process "two systems" (popular decision science) vs single-valuation neuroscience.**
- *What disagrees:* the System 1/System 2 narrative (02 §2) implies two competing modules; neuroscience (04 §2) finds a single valuation signal with hyperbolic discounting (Kable & Glimcher), no literal warring systems.
- *Why:* a useful behavioral metaphor vs a mechanistic account.
- *Current evidence:* dual-process as *description* `[B]`; as *architecture* `[C]`.
- *Uncertainty:* the true computational structure of valuation.

## Section 3 · Assumptions We Should Remove

Listed **only** where current evidence weakens them. Each is an assumption our thinking should no longer rest on.

1. **"Motivation explains behavior."** — Weakened by the intention–behavior gap (02 §16; 01 §10). Motivation is necessary, not explanatory-sufficient.
2. **"Willpower is a depletable resource."** — The strong ego-depletion model failed a 23-lab replication (02 §8; 00 §4). `[D]`
3. **"More reminders/nudges create better behavior."** — Broad nudge effects are small and publication-bias-inflated (02 §6); notification fatigue is real. Only defaults are robust.
4. **"Identity change is sufficient (or the prime cause) for behavior change."** — Behavior→identity is better supported; habits can bypass identity entirely (03 §3; 04 §6). `[C]`
5. **"Streaks improve adherence."** — No direct evidence for durable automaticity; conflicts with recovery evidence and overjustification (03 §5; 01 §3). `[C/D]`
6. **"Habits form on a fixed timeline (≈21 days)."** — Median ~66 days, range 18–254 (03 §4). `[D]` for any fixed number.
7. **"Consistency must be perfect / never miss."** — Missing single occasions did not materially impair formation (03 §4). `[B]`
8. **"Users decide rationally in the moment and can simply 'engage' deliberation."** — Control is state-dependent and degrades under stress/sleep-loss/load; the two-system "switch" is a contested metaphor (04 §3–§5; 02 §2). `[A/B]`
9. **"Acquisition tactics also maintain behavior."** — Maintenance is theoretically distinct from acquisition (03 §7). `[B]`
10. **"Neuro-language ('dopamine hits', 'rewire your brain', 'reset dopamine') is a valid basis for design."** — These are myths/commercial simplifications (04 §1, §10). `[D]`

## Section 4 · Highest-Confidence Principles (candidate scientific foundations)

Ten principles with the greatest explanatory power across all four documents. **Not product ideas** — candidate foundations, each a compression of converging evidence.

1. **The bottleneck is enactment, not desire.** Wanting/deciding rarely translates straight into doing. `[A/B]`
2. **Most behavior is automatic and cue-driven.** Deliberation is the exception, not the rule. `[B]`
3. **Environment and cues are primary levers** — often more reliable than changing minds. `[A/B]`
4. **Self-control is state-dependent.** Stress, sleep loss, and cognitive load degrade prefrontal control and tilt behavior toward habit. `[A/B]`
5. **The immediate is over-weighted** (present bias), and this is neurally grounded — the Present/Future gap is real. `[B]`
6. **Motivational pull dissociates from benefit** — "wanting" ≠ "liking"; people are pulled toward what they won't enjoy. `[A]`
7. **If-then implementation intentions reliably convert intention into action.** `[A/B]`
8. **Self-efficacy and attributable small wins sustain effort**, especially after setbacks. `[A/B]`
9. **How a lapse is construed governs relapse; self-compassion/autonomy support outperform shame/pressure for durability.** `[B]`
10. **Learning runs on prediction error and reference points** — expectation and framing shape the behavioral effect of any outcome. `[A]`

## Section 5 · Greatest Unknowns (ranked by importance to Impulse)

1. **Does app/AI-delivered coaching change real behavior durably?** No direct evidence; the entire thesis rests on an unproven transfer (04 §10; 03 §9). *If one study, this.*
2. **What actually drives recovery/resumption after a lapse?** No validated standalone model exists; evidence is borrowed and mostly lab/single-study (03 §6; 04 post-lapse unknown).
3. **Does the working-alliance / autonomy-support mechanism survive the human→AI medium?** (Bridges to the not-yet-reviewed `05`.) Core to whether coaching can work at all.
4. **What maintains behavior long-term, as distinct from starting it?** Maintenance is under-theorized and long-horizon data are scarce (03 §7).
5. **Can we detect state (stress/sleep/load) unobtrusively, and does state predict lapse risk?** State-dependence is a top principle but its real-time detection is unproven (04 §4–§5).
6. **Does approach-framing (toward the Future Self) beat avoidance-framing on real decisions?** (01 §8.) Testable, unresolved.
7. **Does identity framing add anything over good implementation intentions, or is it redundant?** (01 §4; 03 §3.)
8. **Can a deliberative decision be distinguished from an automatic habit in real time?** Determines which intervention even applies (02 §15).

## Section 6 · Where Existing Products Appear Inconsistent With the Evidence

Criticisms only; **no features proposed.** Each is a gap between common practice and cited evidence.

- **Streak-centric habit trackers** make an unbroken chain the goal — the exact all-or-nothing construal that the abstinence-violation effect links to full relapse (03 §6), and a form of external reward that risks crowding intrinsic motivation (01 §3). `[B]`
- **Notification/reminder-heavy apps** assume more prompts → more behavior; broad nudge effects are small and inflated by publication bias, and only defaults are robust (02 §6). `[C]`
- **Motivation/inspiration-first products** target wanting while the actual bottleneck is enactment (02 §16; 01 §10). `[A/B]`
- **Points/badges gamification** relies on tangible extrinsic rewards that can undermine intrinsic motivation on already-valued behavior (01 §3; Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999). `[B]`
- **"21-day" / fixed-length challenge programs** assert a timeline the evidence contradicts (median ~66 days, range 18–254; 03 §4). `[D]` for the fixed claim.
- **Rational-user design** (assuming the user decides calmly and can "just" deliberate) ignores state-dependence — control collapses under stress/sleep-loss/load exactly when the app is most needed (04 §3–§5). `[A/B]`
- **Neuro-marketing claims** ("dopamine detox," "train/rewire your brain," "dopamine hits") rest on myths and commercial simplifications (04 §1, §10). `[D]`
- **One-size-fits-all goals/timelines** ignore the large individual variance in habit-formation time (03 §4). `[B]`
- **Tough-love / shame-based framing** contradicts the recovery evidence that self-compassion increases, not decreases, subsequent effort (03 §6; Breines & Chen 2012). `[B]`

## Section 7 · Research Agenda — recommended order for Documents 05–10

The original order was 05 → 06 → 07 → 08 → 09 → 10. I recommend re-sequencing to **05 → 08 → 07 → 06 → 09 → 10**, ordered by *risk to the thesis*, then *leverage*, then *foundational consolidation*, then *synthesis*.

1. **05 Coaching Science — first.** The single greatest unknown (Section 5 #1/#3) is whether the coaching *mechanism itself* (alliance, autonomy-support, evocation) has evidence and can plausibly transfer to a non-human medium. If it cannot, much of the rest is downstream of a shaky premise. Reviewing it first tells us whether we are building on rock or sand.
2. **08 Behavior Change Models — second.** This operationalizes the strongest, most cross-disciplinary consensus (Section 1: enactment-not-desire, implementation intentions, environment). It converts the highest-confidence principles into the models the founders will actually reason with. High leverage, high certainty.
3. **07 Emotional Regulation — third.** State-dependence is a top-4 principle and the direct mechanism of the hot moment; regulating the "hot" state is central to the Present/Future gap. It also resolves the emotion strands left open by 02 §9 and 04 §7.
4. **06 Human Needs — fourth.** Important but the lowest marginal value now: it substantially overlaps motivation dynamics already reviewed in 01, and its main contribution (the autonomy/competence/relatedness convergence) is already partly captured. Better placed after the higher-leverage, higher-uncertainty topics.
5. **09 Contradictions — fifth.** Should build *on top of the full topic set* (this document, 12, only covers 01–04). Sequencing it after 05–08 lets it extend this review across the complete corpus rather than duplicate it.
6. **10 Research Synthesis — last**, as designed — it can only synthesize what precedes it.

*Reasoning in one line:* front-load the topic that most threatens the whole enterprise (does coaching work at all?), then the topic that most consolidates certainty (behavior-change models), then the hot-state mechanism, then foundational consolidation, then whole-corpus contradiction and synthesis.
